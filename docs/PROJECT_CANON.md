# PMBaseline Project Canon

**Status:** Foundation v0.1  
**Canonical repository:** `peterpielaetstrayer/PMBaselineMVP`  
**Last updated:** 2026-06-13

## 1. Product definition

PMBaseline is an AI-supported behavior-change and self-regulation system that helps a person notice their current state, understand what their baseline requires, choose an appropriate next action, and learn over time what helps them remain stable and capable.

It is not primarily a habit tracker, fitness dashboard, productivity system, or therapy chatbot. Those tools can contribute data or techniques, but the product's central job is different:

> Help the user make the next baseline-preserving or baseline-restoring move that fits their actual condition today.

The broader method may be called **The Baseline Method**. PMBaseline is the digital application and check-in system through which a user practices and learns the method.

## 2. The problem

People often know many healthy behaviors but struggle to choose and perform the right one when they are tired, anxious, overwhelmed, off routine, recovering from a setback, or attempting too many changes at once.

Existing products commonly create one or more problems:

- They measure a narrow domain rather than the whole context of the day.
- They prescribe the same target regardless of the user's condition.
- They reward completion but do not teach self-regulation.
- They can turn missed habits into shame, avoidance, or abandonment.
- They collect data without translating it into a realistic next move.
- They optimize performance when the user first needs stabilization.

PMBaseline should convert context into appropriate support without removing the user's autonomy.

## 3. Core promise

PMBaseline helps the user answer four questions:

1. **Where am I right now?**
2. **What mode am I in?**
3. **What is the smallest meaningful move available?**
4. **What can I learn from what happens next?**

## 4. Baseline modes

The modes are adaptive states, not personality labels or clinical diagnoses.

### Stabilize

The user is depleted, dysregulated, overwhelmed, at risk of making the situation worse, or unable to carry normal expectations.

The goal is protection and immediate stabilization: food, water, sleep, safety, reduced stimulation, support, avoiding harmful behavior, and completing only essential obligations.

### Rebuild

The user has enough capacity for gentle structure but should avoid an aggressive optimization plan.

The goal is momentum through minimum viable actions: a short walk, basic meal, one work block, light cleaning, a calming routine, or reconnecting with a known stabilizer.

### Maintain

The user is near their functional baseline and can sustain ordinary routines.

The goal is consistency across the user's chosen anchors, obligations, recovery, and relationships.

### Expand

The user is stable enough to take on growth, challenge, deeper learning, creative work, or ambitious training.

The goal is deliberate expansion without sacrificing the conditions that support the baseline.

## 5. Foundational design principles

### Guide, do not punish

The system should reduce shame, pressure, and all-or-nothing thinking. Missing an action is information, not moral failure.

### Minimum viable action

On difficult days, the correct action may be smaller than the user's original plan. A run may become a walk; a workout may become mobility; a full workday may become one essential submission.

### One next move

The application should resist presenting a long optimization checklist when the user has limited capacity. It should identify the most useful next action and allow the user to choose an alternative.

### Structure with freedom

The user needs enough structure to reduce decision fatigue, but enough agency to adapt the method to their life. Suggestions should normally be framed as choices, not commands.

### Teach the method

The AI should not become permanently indispensable. The experience should help users become better at recognizing their own states, selecting interventions, and designing personal systems.

### Context before score

A number without context is weak. A day with no workout may still represent meaningful progress if the user completed an urgent obligation, ate, avoided alcohol, and protected sleep.

### Protect tomorrow

The system should consider downstream effects. Sometimes the highest-value action is ending the day calmly rather than adding another accomplishment.

### Progressive personalization

The system should begin with a small number of universal questions and gradually learn the user's stabilizers, destabilizers, preferences, responsibilities, constraints, and successful patterns.

### Ethical support

The application must not pose as a clinician, diagnose conditions, encourage dependence, or use manipulative engagement mechanics. It should recognize situations that require human or emergency support.

## 6. Baseline Method knowledge layer

The product should eventually teach and operationalize:

- What a personal baseline is and is not
- Physical and mental baseline awareness
- Three Daily Anchors
- Weekly rhythms
- Baseline choice menus
- Minimum viable actions
- Hard Day, Travel Day, and Reset methods
- The psychology of momentum
- Reflection and pattern recognition
- A 30-day baseline map
- Optional modules such as Alcohol Reset and Walk Instead
- Expansion beyond maintenance into growth and meaningful work

This knowledge should appear through short contextual explanations, deeper Learn content, and links from relevant moments in the user's flow.

## 7. Primary experience loop

1. **Notice:** Complete a low-friction check-in.
2. **Interpret:** See the current baseline mode and a short explanation.
3. **Choose:** Select a next best move from one primary recommendation and a small choice menu.
4. **Act:** Perform or modify the action.
5. **Reflect:** Record whether it helped, hurt, or revealed something.
6. **Learn:** Update the user's personal baseline model and future recommendations.

## 8. Example of a successful day

A user begins slowly, feels substantial stress, considers a long walk and a workout, but completes and submits important schoolwork, eats a protein-rich meal, does not drink despite urges, returns home, and chooses meditation, a calming bedtime routine, and a reasonable bedtime.

The application should not classify the day as a failure because exercise was skipped. It should recognize the protected behaviors, reinforce the sober evening and sleep plan, and avoid adding unnecessary goals.

## 9. Initial user groups

The design must serve at least three starting conditions:

1. **Off-baseline user:** needs stabilization and simple support.
2. **Developing user:** has some positive habits but struggles with consistency, prioritization, or setbacks.
3. **Established user:** already maintains many healthy behaviors and wants deeper awareness, experimentation, and refinement.

Onboarding should discover the user's starting condition without stereotyping or forcing a fixed path.

## 10. Product success

The MVP succeeds when users report that it helps them:

- make a better next decision on a difficult day;
- recover from disruption without abandoning the whole system;
- identify personal patterns they did not previously see;
- reduce all-or-nothing behavior;
- maintain or rebuild a small number of meaningful anchors;
- rely gradually more on learned self-regulation rather than constant AI direction.

Engagement alone is not success. Healthy disengagement, reduced prompting needs, and improved self-direction can also represent successful outcomes.

## 11. Non-negotiables

- User data belongs to the user.
- Sensitive data must be protected by default.
- The user must be able to inspect, correct, export, and delete remembered information.
- AI inferences must be distinguishable from user-stated facts.
- High-risk health or safety situations must not be handled as ordinary coaching.
- No leaderboard or social pressure should be introduced without evidence that it supports the method.
- The product should remain usable when the user is stressed, tired, or half-awake.
